The Patākā
in the Gaṇitakaumudī

Prasad Jawalgekar · Bhumika Mittal · K. Ramasubramanian · Aalok Thakkar

Through my interactions with Prof. Aalok, I first encountered this intriguing grid from Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita's Gaṇitakaumudī. Many scholars have studied this text over the years and interpreted several of its mathematical constructions, but this particular formation remained surprisingly mysterious.

The full text surfaced only in the twentieth century, when Padmākara Dvivedī found a complete manuscript in his father Sudhākara's collection and printed it in two volumes, 1936 and 1942.

It appears in Chapter 13, Aṅkapāśa, the chapter devoted entirely to combinatorics. The chapter opens with a rather bold declaration: that this subject delights mathematicians while trapping incompetent ones. What I find even more interesting is what comes immediately afterwards. Nārāyaṇa says that these methods have applications in drama, prosody, medicine, garland-making, mathematics, and architecture, yet he never explains why. He leaves those connections for the reader to discover.
Aṅkapāśa

अथ गणकानन्दकरं सङ्क्षेपादङ्कपाशकं वक्ष्ये ।
निपतन्ति यत्र मत्सरवन्तो दुष्टाः कुगणका ये ॥

atha gaṇakānandakaraṃ saṅkṣepādaṅkapāśakaṃ vakṣye | nipatanti yatra matsaravanto duṣṭāḥ kugaṇakā ye ||

I now introduce aṅkapāśa, which delights mathematicians, and where the jealous, wicked, and incompetent (kugaṇaka) suffer a fall. A pun, śleṣa: the subject of combinatorics, and a net of numbers that traps bad mathematicians.

He then lists where it applies, across six different fields:

dramatics bharata prosody chandaḥśāstra medicine vaidya garland-making mālyakriyā mathematics gaṇita architecture śilpa

And that naturally led us to two questions. First, what exactly does this structure represent? And second, could we reconstruct the algorithm that Nārāyaṇa had in mind? The first question gives us a modern combinatorial interpretation. The second takes us back to the Sanskrit verses. In effect, the talk has two acts: understanding the mathematics, and understanding the Sanskrit algorithm.
Two Questions
How is it built?

A construction for any antima m and sthāna n, not just m = n = 3.

What does it mean?

What the entries are, and why the flag takes this shape.

The text begins by introducing three auxiliary sequences. The first is the Guṇottara sequence, which is simply the powers of m.
The Guṇottara Sequence

आदौ रूपं विलिखेत् अन्तिमगुणितं पुरः पुनस्तद्वत् ।
स्थानाधिकं तु यावत् पङ्क्तिर्गुणकोत्तराख्येयम् ॥

ādau rūpaṃ vilikhet antimaguṇitaṃ puraḥ punastadvat | sthānādhikaṃ tu yāvat paṅktirguṇakottarākhyeyam |

Powers of m:  G(m, n) = 1, m, m2, …, mn.

Finally, the Nārācikā sequence determines the height of each column. Interestingly, these heights are exactly the coefficients of (1 + x + ... + x^(m-1))^n.
The Nārācikā Sequence

अन्तिममितवैश्लेषस्थानाङ्कमिताश्च ताः पृथक् स्थाप्याः ।
तासां घातः सूचीपङ्क्तिर्नाराचिका वा स्यात् ॥

antimamitavaiśleṣasthānāṅkamitāśca tāḥ pṛthak sthāpyāḥ | tāsāṃ ghātaḥ sūcīpaṅktir nārācikā vā syāt |

Coefficients of (1 + x + ⋯ + xm−1)n.

For m = n = 3:  (1 + x + x²)³ = 1 + 3x + 6x² + 7x³ + 6x⁴ + 3x⁵ + x⁶,  so N = 1, 3, 6, 7, 6, 3, 1.

So once these three ingredients are known, only one question remains: how do we fill the rest of the grid? This is the part which is not discussed in the commentaries of Paramanand Singh.

At first glance the entries appear almost random. But after trying several possibilities, one remarkably simple pattern emerged. Take each number, subtract one, write it in base m, and compute the sum of its digits. Then group together all numbers having the same digit sum, and suddenly the entire Patākā assembles itself.
Subtract One, Write in Base Three

the numbers 1 to 27

each number, less one, in three base-three digits · 1 becomes 000, 27 becomes 222

add the three digits · the digit sum runs from 0 to 6

group by digit sum, keeping each group in increasing order

the patākā assembles itself · column heights 1, 3, 6, 7, 6, 3, 1

This also reveals what the Patākā really is. Suppose we have n positions, each of which can contain one of m symbols. There are exactly m^n such sequences. The Patākā is simply a complete enumeration of all of these sequences, grouped according to their digit sum, with the entries within each group appearing in increasing order. Once we realized this interpretation, many features of the construction became immediately clear.

The patākā is an enumeration of [m]n.

All mn ways to fill n places with m symbols, each set in the column of its digit sum.

The same structure surfaces in the algorithm of the verses.

So far, we have uncovered the mathematical meaning of the Patākā. But an equally fascinating question remains: how did Nārāyaṇa actually construct it? Remember, he was not writing down a mathematical proof in the modern sense. He was describing an algorithm entirely in verse. Our task was to reconstruct that procedure and understand why it works.
The Patākā Verses

नाराचपङ्क्त्यङ्कमिताः कोष्ठानामूर्ध्वपङ्क्तयः । तिर्यग्गामी च सर्वासां स्वस्वखण्डावसानमा ॥
पङ्क्तौ तदाद्यकोष्ठे यः पल्लवोऽथाङ्कयोजनाः । तिर्यक्स्थितायामाद्यायां पङ्क्तिमाभ्यासिकीं लिखेत् ॥

तदन्तिमाङ्कः क्षेपाख्यः पुरःस्थः साध्यनामकः । क्षेपं पुरातनैरङ्कैः क्रमात् संयोजयेत् पृथक् ॥
तानधस्तिर्यग्गायां च कोष्ठपङ्क्त्यां विनिक्षिपेत् । साध्याङ्कस्य पताका स्यात् साध्यं क्षेपं प्रकल्पयेत् ॥

साध्यं पुरःस्थितं कृत्वा क्षेपं प्राग्वत् पुरातनैः । अङ्कैराद्यद्वितीयादिकोष्ठपङ्क्तिगतैर्युतम् ॥
तिर्यङ्निरङ्ककोष्ठेषु साङ्काधःस्थेषु विन्यसेत् । येनाङ्केन युतः क्षेपः साध्याङ्कादधिको यदा ॥
तदा मुक्त्वा तमङ्कं तु योजयेदितरांस्ततः ॥

The verses name a few quantities that drive the procedure: purātanāṅka, an entry already placed; kṣepa, the increment; and sādhya, a bound. Nārāyaṇa's algorithm fills the array one stage at a time. For each new entry, it looks at neighbouring entries that have already been computed and combines information from the auxiliary sequences. At first glance these update rules appear rather mysterious, and it is not immediately obvious why they should produce the numbers we want.
Reading the Verses Again

पुरातनाङ्कः

purātanāṅka

the old number

A number already placed in the flag. Every new entry is built from one.

क्षेपः

kṣepa

the increment, j·3r

The current first-row term. Adding it raises one base-three digit by j, so the sum lands exactly j columns to the right.

साध्यः

sādhya

the bound

The next first-row term. Keep only sums below it: precisely the condition that no carry occurs.

The algorithm begins with exactly the three auxiliary sequences we introduced earlier: the Guṇottara sequence gives the powers of m, the Ābhyāsikī sequence gives the first entry of each column, and the Nārācikā sequence tells us how many entries each column should contain. So at this point we already know where every column starts and how tall each column should be, and the only remaining challenge is filling in the missing entries. Nārāyaṇa fills them stage by stage: for each new entry, add the kṣepa to an earlier entry and place the sum in the next diagonal cell, as long as it stays below the sādhya. Notice that we only ever track three numbers: the kṣepa, the sādhya, and the purātanāṅka. Once we interpret the entries as indices of sequences, everything becomes much more natural. Each step effectively moves from one sequence to the next while preserving the correct digit-sum class, so every index appears exactly once, every column keeps its prescribed height, and the entire Patākā gradually emerges.

Each closed stage is a complete smaller Patākā, nested inside the next. Raising n by one wraps the whole flag inside a larger one, so (3,1) sits inside (3,2), which sits inside (3,3). The construction is genuinely recursive, and each step multiplies the number of cells by m.
One Patākā Inside the Next

By the time the procedure terminates, every number from 1 to m^n has been placed. The remarkable part is that this is not an exhaustive search or a sorting algorithm. Instead, Nārāyaṇa constructs the entire arrangement directly through a sequence of local update rules. Our reconstruction shows that these rules produce exactly the graded enumeration we described earlier, and here it is written as modern pseudocode, running in Θ(n·m^n) time.
The Algorithm, Formally
Patākā(m, n)   ▷ antima m, sthāna n
  A ← ābhyāsikī(m, n)   ▷ first-row values j·m^r
  write A along the first row
  K ← A[2];  S ← A[3]   ▷ kṣepa (increment), sādhya (bound)
  repeat
    for each placed entry p, in order:
      if p + K < S:
        place p + K in the next diagonal cell
          ▷ column of p, shifted right by offset(K)
    K ← S;  S ← next term of A
  until K = m^n
  return the grid

offset(K) = j   where K = j·m^r

Column k lists the indices of [m]n whose base-m digits sum to mn − k, in increasing order.  The whole grid is built in Θ(n · mn) time, optimal for listing all mn indices.

This gives us two complementary ways of understanding the Patākā. From a modern perspective, it is a graded enumeration of all sequences over an alphabet of size m, grouped by digit sum. From Nārāyaṇa's perspective, it is an elegant algorithm that constructs precisely the same object using only a handful of auxiliary sequences and simple iterative rules. Taken together, they reveal both the combinatorial insight and the algorithmic sophistication underlying this remarkable construction. Thank you.
