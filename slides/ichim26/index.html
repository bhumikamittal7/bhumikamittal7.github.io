The full text surfaced only in the twentieth century, when Padmākara Dvivedī found a complete manuscript in his father Sudhākara's collection and printed it in two volumes, 1936 and 1942. Chapter 13 is devoted entirely to combinatorics.

Aṅkapāśa the binding of numbers · Chapter 13

अथ गणकानन्दकरं सङ्क्षेपादङ्कपाशकं वक्ष्ये ।
निपतन्ति यत्र मत्सरवन्तो दुष्टाः कुगणका ये ॥

atha gaṇakānandakaraṃ saṅkṣepādaṅkapāśakaṃ vakṣye | nipatanti yatra matsaravanto duṣṭāḥ kugaṇakā ye ||

I now introduce aṅkapāśa, which delights mathematicians, and where the jealous, wicked, and incompetent (kugaṇaka) suffer a fall. A pun, śleṣa: the subject of combinatorics, and a net of numbers that traps bad mathematicians.

He then lists where it applies, across six different fields:

dramatics bharata prosody chandaḥśāstra medicine vaidya garland-making mālyakriyā mathematics gaṇita architecture śilpa

Two Open Questions

Kusuba and Singh translated the verses and described the finished object. Two questions stayed open, and we look at both:

What does it run?

The verses name parts of a procedure, kṣepa, sādhya, purātanāṅka, but no one had written down the algorithm they describe.

What does it mean?

The flag is full of numbers in a precise arrangement. What are the entries, and why does the shape come out the way it does?

We begin with the finished flag, and look closely.

A Closer Look patterns hiding in the finished flag
  • The first row: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18, 27. Small multiples of powers of three, in increasing order.
  • The column heights: 1, 3, 6, 7, 6, 3, 1. A palindrome, and the seven counts sum to 27.
  • At the corners of the stages: 1, 3, 9, 27. The powers of three.
  • Everything here speaks base three. So subtract one, and write every number in base three.

Subtract One, Write in Base Three every number in the flag, as three base-three digits

the numbers 1 to 27

each number, less one, in three base-three digits · 1 becomes 000, 27 becomes 222

add the three digits · the digit sum runs from 0 to 6

group by digit sum, keeping each group in increasing order

the patākā assembles itself · column heights 1, 3, 6, 7, 6, 3, 1

Each column of the patākā collects the numbers that share one base-three digit sum.

So the flag is an enumeration. Fill n places, each with one of m symbols: there are mn such arrangements. Number them 1 to mn, and the patākā lists every one, sorted into columns by digit sum, and within a column in increasing order.

For antima and sthāna both three, that is all 27 ways to place three symbols in three positions, grouped into seven columns of digit sums 0 through 6. Not a count but a full listing: each arrangement in its place, none missing, none repeated.

Before the flag can even be drawn, one number is needed for each column: how many arrangements share that digit sum? That count is the next sequence.

The Nārācikā Sequence how many numbers share each digit sum

That count, the number of arrangements in each digit-sum column, is what Nārāyaṇa calls the nārācikā, the needle sequence.

अन्तिममितवैश्लेषस्थानाङ्कमिताश्च ताः पृथक् स्थाप्याः ।
तासां घातः सूचीपङ्क्तिर्नाराचिका वा स्यात् ॥

antimamitavaiśleṣasthānāṅkamitāśca tāḥ pṛthak sthāpyāḥ | tāsāṃ ghātaḥ sūcīpaṅktir nārācikā vā syāt |

Their product is the needle sequence, the nārācikā. For m and n equal to three it reads 1, 3, 6, 7, 6, 3, 1: exactly the column heights of the flag.

Deriving the Nārācikā

Nārāyaṇa writes the vaiśleṣiṇī, a row of ones, once for every place, and multiplies the copies together. Each copy is laid one step across the last and the columns are added. For three places, two passes over 1 1 1 give first 1 2 3 2 1, then 1 3 6 7 6 3 1. In modern dress this is polynomial multiplication: the nārācikā is the coefficients of (1 + x + x²)³.

The Ābhyāsikī Sequence the smallest number with each digit sum

स्थानाहतोऽन्तिमाङ्कः सैकः स्थानोनितश्च तच्छेषम् ।
आभ्यासिक्यां पङ्क्तौ प्रजायते स्थानमानमिह ॥

sthānāhato'ntimāṅkaḥ saikaḥ sthānonitaśca taccheṣam | ābhyāsikyāṃ paṅktau prajāyate sthānamānam iha |

Take the caya 1, 2, 3. Drop its first term, then multiply by three again and again.

In base three these are 000, 001, 002, 012, 022, 122, 222, less one each: the smallest number with each digit sum. Pack the twos into the lowest places.

Reading the Verses Again

पुरातनाङ्कः

purātanāṅka

the old number

A number already placed in the flag. Every new entry is built from one.

क्षेपः

kṣepa

the increment, j·3r

The current first-row term. Adding it raises one base-three digit by j, so the sum lands exactly j columns to the right.

साध्यः

sādhya

the bound

The next first-row term. Keep only sums below it: precisely the condition that no carry occurs.

The Patākā Algorithm

Add the kṣepa to the earlier numbers and set each sum in the next diagonal cell, so long as it stays under the sādhya.

When a stage closes, the sādhya becomes the new kṣepa and the next first-row term becomes the new bound. Four stages fill the flag.

The Algorithm, from the Verses the procedure the six and a half verses describe
  1. 1 पल्लव · आभ्यासिकी
    pallava · ābhyāsikī     Write the ābhyāsikī along the first row. Each term is the smallest number of its column.
  2. 2 क्षेपः · साध्यः
    kṣepa · sādhya     Take one first-row term as the increment kṣepa K, and the next as the bound sādhya S.
  3. 3 पुरातनाङ्कः
    purātanāṅka     For each earlier number p already placed, form the sum p + K.
  4. 4 तिर्यक् · निरङ्ककोष्ठ
    tiryak · empty cell     If p + K < S, drop it into the next empty diagonal cell. If p + K ≥ S, discard it and move on.
  5. 5 गुणोत्तरः
    guṇottara     When the stage is spent, S becomes the new K and the next term the new bound. Each time S reaches a power of m, a smaller patākā has closed. Stop at mn.
Why It Works the verses give the how, the digit sum gives the why
What the verses do

Additions, bounded by the sādhya.

A running procedure over the numbers already placed. No notion of a digit sum ever appears in the text.

=one fact links them
What the flag means

A partition of 1 … mn by digit sum.

Column k holds exactly the arrangements whose base-m digits sum to k, in increasing order.

  • Adding a kṣepa j·mr to a number below mr raises a single base-m digit from 0 to j, with no carry, so the digit sum grows by exactly j and the sum moves exactly j columns right.
  • The sādhya bound is precisely the condition "below mr": every guard in the verses is a carry check in disguise.

Every number from 1 to mn is placed exactly once, in the column of its digit sum.

Correct placement, no duplicates, and mn cells filled. The arithmetic and the Sanskrit prove to be the same object.

From Prosody to Any Alphabet
  • c. 300 BCEPiṅgala · Chandaḥśāstra · laghu and guru syllablesm = 2
  • c. 200 BCE to 200 CEBharata · Nāṭyaśāstra · metres and tālasm = 2
  • 7th centuryVirahāṅka · mātrā counts and the two-term recurrencem = 2
  • 13th centuryŚārṅgadeva · Saṅgītaratnākara · four tālāṅgasm = 4
  • 1356Nārāyaṇa · Gaṇitakaumudī · antima and sthāna freeany m, any n

The alphabet itself becomes a parameter. Prosody is m = 2, music is m = 4; the patākā belongs to the general theory that contains them all.

The reconstruction

The first complete algorithm behind the patākā, faithful to the verses: kṣepa, sādhya, and the diagonal placement, read as a running procedure.

2

The meaning

What the entries are: a graded enumeration of 1 to mn by base-m digit sum, each column in increasing order.

The guarantees

Proofs of correctness and of optimality: Θ(n · mn) time, matching the lower bound any listing of mn numbers must pay.
